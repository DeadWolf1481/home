Hagia Sophia: Istanbul's Greatest Monument

📅 April 10, 2026⏱ 6 min read

Few buildings in the world carry as much history as Hagia Sophia. For nearly 1,500 years, this extraordinary structure has stood at the heart of Istanbul — switching empires, religions, and functions, yet never losing its power to astonish.

A Marvel of Byzantine Engineering

Completed in 537 AD under Emperor Justinian I, Hagia Sophia was for nearly a thousand years the largest cathedral in the world. Its central dome — 56 metres high and 31 metres wide — was a feat that architects and engineers of the time considered almost impossible. The massive half-domes and forty windows ringing the base of the main dome create an effect that has been described across the centuries as the dome 'floating' on a halo of light.

From Cathedral to Mosque to Museum — and Back

After the Ottoman conquest of Constantinople in 1453, Sultan Mehmed II converted Hagia Sophia into a mosque. The Byzantine mosaics were plastered over, four minarets were added, and Islamic calligraphy panels were hung inside. The building served as a mosque for nearly 500 years until 1934, when Atatürk transformed it into a secular museum. In 2020, it was reconverted into a functioning mosque, though it remains open to visitors of all backgrounds outside prayer times.

What to See Inside

Despite its complex history, Hagia Sophia's interior is remarkably well-preserved. Look out for the golden Byzantine mosaics — particularly the famous Deësis mosaic depicting Christ flanked by the Virgin Mary and John the Baptist — and the large Ottoman roundels bearing the names of Allah, the Prophet Muhammad, and the first caliphs. The second-floor gallery offers the best vantage point for the main dome.

Visitor Practical Info

Hagia Sophia is located in the Sultanahmet neighbourhood of Istanbul's historic peninsula. As an active mosque, modest dress is required — shoulders and knees should be covered, and women should bring a headscarf. Entry is free. Plan your visit early in the morning to avoid peak crowds, and allow at least 90 minutes to explore properly.
